Why the Standard Matters

Trucking is one of the most dangerous occupations in the United States. OSHA regulates the warehouses, docks, and job sites our drivers deliver to, and the Department of Transportation regulates the miles in between. We build our program around both, because the numbers are not abstract.

11%

Of all U.S. worker deaths in 2017 came from the trucking industry, more than any other occupation

75%+

Of fatally injured truckers died in transportation incidents

#3

Heavy and tractor-trailer drivers rank third highest for nonfatal injury and illness across all occupations

The Four Hazard Areas We Manage

OSHA groups trucking risk into four operational categories. Each one maps directly to a section of our written safety program and our driver training.

01

Highway Driving

Interstate and intrastate operation under DOT hours-of-service rules, driver qualification standards, and defensive driving practice. This is where the majority of industry fatalities occur, and where our training investment is heaviest.

How We Run It

A safety manual on a shelf is paperwork. These are the practices that put it on the road.

Driver Qualification

Class A CDL, clean MVR, and verified experience before a driver ever touches a truck. Ongoing MVR monitoring and DOT physical compliance for every driver in the fleet.

Pre-Trip & Post-Trip Inspection

Documented inspection on every unit, every run. Defects get written up and repaired before dispatch, not after a roadside stop finds them.

In-House Maintenance

Our own shop and our own mechanics on a preventive maintenance schedule. We do not wait for a breakdown to service a truck, and we do not push equipment past interval to make a delivery window.

Load Securement

Chains, straps, tarps, and rigging matched to the load. Oversized and heavy haul moves get a securement plan and permit routing before the truck is loaded.

Hours of Service

Electronic logging with full DOT compliance. Dispatch plans around legal hours. No load is worth a fatigued driver.

Incident Reporting

Every incident and near-miss gets documented and reviewed. What we learn goes back into training instead of into a filing cabinet.
